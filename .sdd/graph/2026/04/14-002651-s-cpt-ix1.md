Contracts in SDD are meant as guardrails for automation and alignment — not as bureaucratic walls that block dialogue-emerged insight. Pre-flight treats them closer to hard limits: when the first attempt to capture `d-prc-22i` (superseding the `d-prc-zch` contract with a directive) was rejected, the validator demanded either a companion contract or a kind-preserving supersede — structurally ceremonious when the substance of the decision was "this rule shouldn't be a graph contract at all." `--skip-preflight` was used after dialogue.

The rigidity isn't consistent with the framework's own principle, already established in `a-tac-eqc`: pre-flight should treat dialogue-sourced reasoning in entry text as human-confirmed context and focus on structural completeness, not re-argue judgment calls. When human and agent have reasoned through a non-standard move (retiring a contract, reshaping a pattern, rejecting ceremony), pre-flight's job is to verify the reasoning is present and coherent, not to gatekeep on form.

The risk of rigidity: SDD starts to feel bureaucratic. Every rare but legitimate move — contract retirement, pattern reshaping, structural experimentation — gets rejected and requires `--skip-preflight`, teaching users to bypass validation rather than engage with it. The guardrail becomes an obstacle.

The gap surfaced here — pre-flight's structural checks don't extend the dialogue-confirmed-context principle already present in the shared `entry_quality` block (from `a-tac-eqc`) to supersede and kind-matching rules. That principle currently covers rationale wording; it does not yet cover structural moves like retirement-without-replacement.
