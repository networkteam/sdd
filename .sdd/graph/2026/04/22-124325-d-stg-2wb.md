SDD emerges from the work itself, not as a separate mode. Participants stay in their natural activity — chatting, coding, designing, running a meeting — and the graph grows from that motion. Tooling minimizes mode-switching; capture is ambient, emerging from ongoing engagement rather than imposed as a parallel chore.

What this aspiration pushes against:
- **Mode-switching** — explicit transitions that take participants out of their work to engage with SDD (tool switches, context gates, formal session starts). The graph should reach into the work, not pull participants out to it.
- **Dedicated SDD sessions** — events scheduled specifically for the graph (planning offsites, graph-review meetings, capture ceremonies). A graph-guided catch-up at the start of a regular meeting, or capture during a design dialogue, stays inside the work; a "quarterly SDD planning offsite" creates parallel activity.

Distinct from the artifacts aspiration (`20260422-123237-d-stg-3k0`) — that pull is *artifact-facing* (no wikis, tickets, dashboards as separate systems); this one is *activity-facing* (no dedicated SDD activities). Together they express "minimal overhead" as two complementary facets: artifact overhead and activity overhead.

Distinct from the dialogue aspiration (`20260422-122136-d-stg-beb`) — dialogue specifies *what* the process is (multi-party engagement); ambient capture adds *where* (embedded in existing work, not in dedicated sessions).

Evidence in `s-cpt-gjs`'s attachment: Kōgen-story examples of capture ambient to work — Jun narrating discovery while cleaning the Probat, morning bar conversations becoming decisions, Priya working through choices during implementation, the analytics agent generating signals from engagement data rather than from a scheduled review.
