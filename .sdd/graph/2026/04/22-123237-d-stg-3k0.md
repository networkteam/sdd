Reasoning lives in one place — the graph, across one loop. Whatever the work produces IS the artifact. Parallel systems of record (wikis, tickets, specs, dashboards, status reports) and ritual ceremony disconnected from the loop aren't needed — the graph is the record, and dialogue (in meetings, chat, code review, wherever it happens) moves it forward.

What this aspiration pushes against:
- **Parallel artifacts** — wikis, tickets, docs, specs, dashboards, status reports maintained alongside the graph; reasoning has to be mirrored and drifts across copies
- **Parallel ceremony** — meetings, rituals, or process gates that run *outside* the reasoning loop (standups because the methodology prescribes them, status reviews that don't draw on or feed back into the graph). A graph-guided catch-up or coordinated dialogue on open signals is *part of* the loop, not parallel to it. Alignment-building time — even when it doesn't produce direct graph entries — is real value, not overhead.

Operational test for proposals: *how much does it let the graph do the work directly, rather than adding a parallel artifact or ritual to bridge the gap?*

Echoes the "each fact lives in exactly one place" principle made explicit as a pull, and operationalizes what the framing directive (`d-stg-0gh`) called "operating without legacy process overhead."

Branches from the dialogue aspiration (`20260422-122136-d-stg-beb`): overcoming parallel artifacts is possible *because* dialogue-in-the-work replaces parallel record-keeping. If decisions don't emerge from dialogue, they have to be documented separately. If they do, the dialogue record IS the artifact.
